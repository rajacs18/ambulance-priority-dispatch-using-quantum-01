Question

Why did you use quantum for this? Can't normal computers do it?

"Normal computers check each possible ambulance assignment one by one. As the city grows — more ambulances, more emergencies — that becomes millions of combinations. Quantum explores all combinations at the same time using superposition. That's the core reason."
Question

What exactly is QAOA doing in your project?

"QAOA takes our dispatch problem, converts it into a format quantum understands, runs it through a quantum circuit, and outputs the best ambulance assignment as a binary answer — like 1010, meaning ambulance 1 goes to emergency 1, ambulance 2 goes to emergency 2."
Question

What is superposition in your context?

"In our project, 4 qubits represent 4 assignment decisions. Superposition means all 16 possible combinations exist simultaneously inside the circuit. QAOA processes all of them at once and picks the best — classical would process them one by one."
Question

How does quantum know which answer is correct?

"We give it a cost function — a formula that scores every possible assignment based on travel time, traffic, and patient severity. Quantum amplifies the low-cost solutions and suppresses the high-cost ones. The answer with highest probability at the end is our optimal dispatch."
Question

Did quantum actually give a better answer than classical here?

"Both gave the same optimal answer — which is actually what we expected at small scale. It validates our quantum model is correct. The real advantage shows when the problem scales to 20-30 ambulances — that's where classical struggles and quantum doesn't."
Question

What is a qubit simply?

"A normal bit is 0 or 1 — like a light switch. A qubit is like a dimmer — it can be 0, 1, or both at the same time until you measure it. That 'both at once' property is what gives quantum its power."
Question

What does the output of your quantum circuit look like?

"It gives a bitstring — like 1010. Each digit maps to one decision. 1 means assigned, 0 means not assigned. We read it and directly know which ambulance goes to which emergency. It also shows a probability chart — the tallest bar is the best solution."
Question

Is this practically usable in real life?

"Not yet in real-time — current quantum hardware is still maturing. But the algorithm and framework we built is production-ready. When quantum hardware scales up in 5-7 years, this exact model can plug directly into a city's emergency dispatch system."
Question

Why not just use Google Maps or a simple app?

"Google Maps finds the shortest route for one vehicle. Our problem is different — multiple ambulances, multiple emergencies, different severity levels, all needing to be optimized together simultaneously. That joint decision-making under constraints is where quantum optimization adds value."
Question

What happens if the quantum answer is wrong?

"QAOA gives probabilities — not a single answer. If the top answer is wrong, the second highest probability answer is the fallback. We also cross-verify against our classical Dijkstra result. In our demo, both matched — so the system is validated."
