🟦 Slide 1 — Title Slide

"Good morning respected judges. We present a quantum optimization framework for emergency ambulance dispatch.

Our objective is to model real-world emergency assignment as a formal optimization problem and solve it using QUBO formulation and QAOA — a hybrid quantum-classical algorithm.

This project demonstrates how quantum computing principles can be applied to real-time logistics and healthcare systems."

⏸ Pause. Look at judges before moving to the next slide.

🟦 Slide 2 — Problem Statement

"In emergency response systems, the key objective is minimizing response time. However, response time is influenced by multiple factors:

  • Distance between ambulance and patient
  • Traffic congestion
  • Patient severity

Additionally, we must ensure each ambulance is assigned to exactly one patient, and each patient is served by exactly one ambulance.

This makes the problem a constrained combinatorial optimization problem."

🟦 Slide 3 — Real-World Complexity

"If there are N ambulances and N patients, there are N! possible assignments:

  • 4 ambulances → 24 possibilities
  • 10 ambulances → 3.6 million possibilities
  • 20 ambulances → 2.4 quintillion possibilities

This factorial growth makes classical brute-force infeasible at scale. This is where quantum optimization becomes relevant."

💡 If a judge interrupts here — good. It shows engagement. Welcome the question.

🟦 Slide 4 — Our Cost Model

"We define cost as:

Cost = Distance × Traffic × Severity

Distance represents base travel time. Traffic models congestion multiplier. Severity ensures critical patients are prioritized. This ensures high-severity patients increase effective cost if delayed, guiding the optimizer toward prioritization."

🔑 Key line: "This converts a real-world decision problem into a measurable optimization objective."

🟦 Slide 5 — Mathematical Formulation

"We define binary decision variables:

  • x(i,j) = 1 if ambulance i is assigned to patient j
  • x(i,j) = 0 otherwise

Constraints: each ambulance assigned exactly once, each patient assigned exactly once. This structure naturally forms an assignment optimization problem."

🟦 Slide 6 — Why QUBO?

"To use quantum optimization algorithms, we convert the constrained problem into QUBO form — Quadratic Unconstrained Binary Optimization.

In QUBO, the objective function includes cost terms and constraints are converted into penalty terms. This creates a single quadratic energy function suitable for quantum optimization."

If judge asks "Why QUBO?":
"Because QAOA and quantum annealing operate naturally on quadratic binary formulations."

🟦 Slide 7 — Why QAOA?

"QAOA stands for Quantum Approximate Optimization Algorithm. It is a hybrid algorithm that uses a parameterized quantum circuit, evaluates cost expectation, and updates parameters using a classical optimizer.

It is specifically designed for combinatorial optimization problems such as MaxCut and assignment problems."

🔑 Important clarification: "Currently, we run QAOA on a quantum simulator." Be honest — judges respect transparency.

🟦 Slide 8 — System Architecture

"The workflow is:

User inputs → Cost matrix → QUBO formulation → QAOA solver → Optimal assignment → Visualization

We also compute the classical brute-force solution for baseline comparison."

💡 This shows scientific rigor — they will appreciate you having a comparison baseline.

🟦 Slide 9 — Implementation

"We implemented using Python, Qiskit, FastAPI, and a JavaScript dashboard for live visualization.

The QUBO conversion is handled using the Qiskit Optimization module. The QAOA algorithm runs with a classical COBYLA optimizer. The website communicates with our Python engine via REST API."

🟦 Slide 10 — Live Demonstration

"This graph shows distances between ambulances and patients — colored green to red based on distance. The optimal assignment is determined by minimizing total cost across all constraint factors."

🔑 Very important line: "We are not hiding complexity inside the quantum algorithm — everything is mathematically verifiable." Judges love this.

🟦 Slide 11 — Classical vs Quantum

"For small problem sizes, classical brute-force is faster. This is expected.

However, classical complexity grows factorially with scale. Quantum optimization is expected to scale differently and may provide advantage at larger combinatorial instances."

If judge says "But here classical is faster":
"Yes, because this is a small-scale simulation. On a quantum simulator, QAOA takes longer because simulating qubits on classical hardware is expensive. On real quantum hardware, this tradeoff reverses at scale."

Stay calm. Confidence matters.

🟦 Slide 12 — Scalability

"At large scale, classical brute-force grows factorially while quantum optimization potentially scales polynomially or approximately.

However, current quantum hardware is limited in qubits and noise tolerance. Therefore, this project demonstrates feasibility and modeling rather than immediate quantum supremacy."

🔑 Strong sentence: "This project demonstrates feasibility and modeling rather than immediate quantum supremacy."

🟦 Slide 13 — Limitations

"Current limitations include:

  • Running on a classical quantum simulator
  • Limited qubit availability on real hardware
  • Noise in real quantum devices
  • Small problem size for demonstration purposes

We are fully transparent about these constraints."

💡 Transparency = Credibility. Judges trust honest teams more than teams that overclaim.
